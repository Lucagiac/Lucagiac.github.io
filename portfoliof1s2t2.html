The Dynamic Street

a story on the future of architecture, with a focus on the final result of a process during which I was a bit out of my comfort zone

written in Spring 2018, settled in Toronto, Canada
“What if we could create a streetscape that responds to citizens’ needs - hence changing dynamically?” Carlo Ratti_

The Dynamic Street, a prototype of a modular and reconfigurable paving system which hints at the possibility of the streetscape seamlessly adapting to people’s needs. In order to reconfigure the streetscape for multiple uses, the project explores the different patterns that can be created on the hexagonal grid as well as the integration of lights into individual pavers.

4 minutes read

revolutioning streetscape design

Commissioned by Alphabet's subsidiary Sidewalk Labs, the project represented a chance for experimenting with a "living lab" at 307, Sidewalk Lab’s office and experimentation space in Toronto.

Imagine an urban street, nestled between buildings with mostly foot and bicycle traffic. During the morning and evening hours, there might be a steady stream of commuters heading to work. In the middle of the day and the evening, families might use the street as a play space. And on the weekend, the street could be cleared for a block party or a basketball game.

The Dynamic Street features a series of hexagonal modular pavers which can be picked up and replaced, according to the final use. We created a taxonomy of different materials and finishes for such pavers, as shown below. Each one of the pavers comprehends a metal plug with a built-in LED, a paver base and a paver topper.

The project resulted in a vast true scale prototype of about 200 square meters, where visitors could engage with Sidewalk Labs, with designers and eventually with the municipality in order to collaboratively think about the future of Toronto.

A combination of physical cues (the street furnitures and paver toppers) combined with the built-in LEDS would help users understand how the space was changing.

On top of the paver, we also investigated how different plug&play elements could contribute to assigning new functions to the space.

How could users be involved in re-shaping the streetscape? On the one hand, visitors could engage with the paver-picking robot. On the other hand, everybody could load "new configurations" from a tablet interface. Users could load several scenarios (i.e. 1 car + 2 bikes lanes) or literally draw live new proposals. In that way, the feedback was of immediate understanding to most.

how we got there

The first mockups were very conceptual and low-fidelity, still helping the team to develop and finetune the concept. Soon we went on with experimenting on materials, going beyond rubber and wood. We had a lot of fun with resins!

Engineering-wise, the mechanical plug was surely the most challenging aspect. It was meant to be robust, to accommodate a LED and a street furniture piece - such as a bollard.

Quickly after testing on full scale mockups in Italy, the team moved to Toronto, where we started to construct the full scale set-up, enjoying the breeze coming from Lake Ontario.

That's how we created a dynamically changing streetscape in Toronto.

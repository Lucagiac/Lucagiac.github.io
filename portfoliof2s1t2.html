take me back

Zona Martiska

a story on human-robot dialogues, with a focus on the design process during which I was a bit out of my comfort zone

written in Fall 2019, settled in Turin, Italy
“Music exists in time; architecture, instead, does exist in space. Dance lives altogether in space and time.” Curt Sachs_

Zona Martiska consists of an immersive performance in which all participants share the intimacy of a collective urban exploration. Freely inspired by "Stalker" of A.Tarkovskij, the performance guides the audience through unexplored and abandoned architectural pieces. Thanks to a robot and sensors on the dancers' body, the result is a unique and site-specific performance.

4 minutes read

how it started

The beginning of the journey was quite unexpected: six friends with different backgrounds - from design to composing music - teamed up to participate in a public call of Compagnia di San Paolo. The aim of the scholarship was to support artistic production of a concept coming from a team of under-30 kids. Suprise, we won the prize and got to work!

All in all we knew where we wanted to go: merging architecture with dance, through the use of technology. And that is what we started to research, opening a call for three dancers and a choreographer.

We quickly identified two components. On the one hand, we wanted a sort of an interactive robot to scan the space, measuring distances and turning them into a soundscape that interacts with the moving audience. On the other hand, performers were to be equipped with body sensors that translate their movement into sound modulations.

reharsing and researching

After putting together the team, we launched three residence programs in which we investigated both the technological and the coreographical aspects of the soon-to-be performance. Performers were equipped with a Nintendo Wii - a very precise and easy to hack IMU sensor, which could talk to Max Msp via a software called Osculator.

So how did it work? Each one of the performer could generate and modulate a specific musical instrument, according to the position of her arm - rotation, pitch and accelleration were the key parameters.

At the same time, Martino - the cute scanning robot - came to life and started to detect presence of walls, obstacles and people while rotating on its body. The distance detected would be transformed into a sound modulation.

Martino got then upgraded to a real PLA body, which gave him a total italian classy look.

Parallel to that, we also kept on investigating what was the other core part of the performance: architecture. We were looking for abandoned places and were keen on using our performance as an excuse to bring in audience to places which are not accessible. Luckily, the region of Piedmont opened for us the door of the greenhouse at Parco La Mandria -abandoned since 1998 - right in the outskirt of Turin.

Needless to say, we were happy as kids in front of such a pristine and desolated landscape - it was the right venue for our performance.

3-2-1 live!

So it happened, on a three days palimpsest we launced six shows of about fifty minutes. At each time, only 50 visitors were allowed, cause we wanted it to be an intimate experience.

Martino the robot scanner also got quite an upgrade: it is now equipped with a 40m range sensor and a laser scanner which shows the direction of the sensing unit.

The video below shows an extract from the performance, enjoy it!

To conclude we received plenty of positive reactions. However, as expected, several people found the interaction too difficult to be understood - surely something to work on for the next performances!

That's how we created a Zona Martiska, an interactive performance in Turin, Italy.
